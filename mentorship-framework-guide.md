# GROW Mentorship Framework Guide

## Overview

The **GROW** coaching model is one of the world's most widely used mentorship and coaching frameworks. Developed in the 1980s by Sir John Whitmore and his colleagues at Performance Consultants International, it was first published in *Coaching for Performance* (1988). The model gives mentors and mentees a clear, structured way to navigate goal-setting conversations so that insight turns into follow-through.

The name **GROW** is an acronym that stands for four sequential phases:

| Phase | Focus | Purpose |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **G — Goal** | Aspirations & desired outcomes | Clarify what the mentee truly wants to achieve |
| **R — Reality** | Current situation & obstacles | Understand where the mentee stands right now and what barriers exist |
| **O — Options** | Strengths, resources & strategies | Brainstorm multiple possible paths forward |
| **W — Way Forward** | Accountability & action | Commit to specific next steps — what, when, by whom, and the will to do it |

---

## Phase-by-Phase Guide

### 1. Goal

**Purpose:** Define a clear, inspiring, and challenging goal.

- Begin with broad aspirations and narrow down to a specific, measurable target.
- Ensure the goal is something the mentee *owns* — not imposed by the mentor.
- Well-set goals are both inspirational and achievable; they create a personal connection that fuels motivation.

**Sample questions:**
- "What would you like to achieve from our mentoring relationship?"
- "What does success look like for you in 6–12 months?"
- "If you could accomplish one breakthrough this year, what would it be?"
- "How will you know you've reached your goal?"

### 2. Reality

**Purpose:** Honestly assess the current landscape — strengths, constraints, and emotions.

- Explore the mentee's present circumstances without judgment.
- Identify internal and external obstacles.
- Connect the goal to deeper personal motivation; understanding *why* a goal matters sustains effort when things get hard.

**Sample questions:**
- "Where are you right now relative to that goal?"
- "What have you already tried? What happened?"
- "What strengths do you have that could help you?"
- "What's holding you back — internally or externally?"
- "How important is this goal to you on a scale of 1–10?"

### 3. Options

**Purpose:** Generate a rich menu of possible strategies before narrowing down.

- Encourage creative, even unconventional, thinking — "what if budget and resources were unlimited?"
- Do NOT evaluate or judge options during brainstorming; quantity first, quality later.
- Consider who could help, what resources exist, and what new skills might be needed.

**Sample questions:**
- "What could you do to move forward?"
- "What else?" *(keep asking — the best ideas often come after the obvious ones)*
- "If you had unlimited resources, what would you try?"
- "Who could support you? What skills or knowledge would you need to acquire?"
- "What would you advise a friend facing the same situation?"

### 4. Way Forward

**Purpose:** Turn the best options into a concrete, accountable action plan.

- Select one or two high-commitment options.
- Define specific actions: *what* will be done, *when*, and *by whom*.
- Gauge the mentee's willingness (the "Will") — a plan without genuine commitment won't survive contact with reality.
- Set check-in dates for follow-through.

**Sample questions:**
- "Which option or options will you commit to?"
- "What exactly will you do, and by when?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how committed are you to this action?"
- "What could get in the way, and how will you handle it?"
- "When should we check in on progress?"

## Tips for Mentors

1. **Spend most time on Goal & Reality.** Deeper exploration in these phases creates stronger vision and motivation, making the later phases almost self-directing.
2. **The model is not rigidly linear.** It's perfectly valid to loop back — revisiting the Goal after exploring Reality often sharpens commitment.
3. **You are not the answer-provider.** The GROW model is built on the belief that mentees are resourceful. Your job is to ask powerful questions, not to prescribe solutions.
4. **Listen more than you speak.** The best mentoring conversations are 70–80% mentee voice.
5. **Celebrate small wins.** Accountability isn't just about tracking gaps — acknowledge progress to sustain motivation.

## Variations & Extensions

| Variation | Addition | Use case |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **TGROW** | Adds a **Topic** phase before Goal | When the mentee isn't sure what to focus on yet |
| **GROWTH** | Adds **Tactics** (specific steps) and **Habits** (daily practices) | When goals need more operational detail and long-term sustainability |

## Applying GROW to Asian Entrepreneur Mentorship

For young Asian entrepreneurs, the GROW framework can be especially powerful when adapted to address common cultural dynamics:

- **Goal:** Many Asian entrepreneurs face family or community expectations around "safe" career paths (medicine, law, finance). Use the Goal phase to help mentees articulate *their own* vision, separate from inherited expectations.
- **Reality:** Acknowledge cultural pressures honestly — family obligations, societal stigma around failure, limited access to role models. Naming these obstacles reduces their power.
- **Options:** Encourage mentees to consider hybrid or non-traditional paths. The entrepreneurial journey rarely follows a straight line; brainstorming unconventional options is where creativity lives.
- **Way Forward:** Build accountability structures that respect cultural context — e.g., framing progress in ways that families can appreciate, or finding peer circles of fellow Asian entrepreneurs for mutual support.
