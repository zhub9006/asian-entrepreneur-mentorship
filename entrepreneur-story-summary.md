## The Non-Traditional Path

Nadya Okamoto's journey is a textbook example of a non-traditional career path — one that began not in a classroom or a corporate internship, but in a moment of personal crisis and empathy.

### The Spark (Age 16)

While her family experienced housing instability and she had to commute long distances to school, Nadya noticed that many homeless women lacked access to menstrual products. This wasn't an abstract policy issue — it was something she witnessed daily. At 16, she founded **PERIOD**, a youth-run nonprofit dedicated to distributing menstrual hygiene products and advocating to end the "tampon tax." Instead of following the conventional high-school-to-college-to-corporate pipeline, she chose to lead a national movement.

### Building PERIOD

PERIOD grew into one of the largest youth-led nonprofits in the United States, with chapters on hundreds of college campuses. Nadya organized product drives, lobbied legislators, and built a community of young advocates — all while still a student at Harvard. She wasn't waiting for permission or credentials; she was building credibility through action.

### Writing *Period Power*

After PERIOD, Nadya expanded her platform by writing **Period Power: A Manifesto for the Menstrual Movement** (2020). The book connected period stigma to broader issues of gender equity, healthcare access, and cultural silence — themes that resonate especially strongly in many Asian cultures where menstruation is often taboo. Becoming an author at such a young age was another non-traditional move: she used storytelling as a tool for advocacy, not just as a personal brand exercise.

### Co-founding August

In 2021, Nadya co-founded **August**, a next-generation menstrual care brand built around sustainability, inclusivity, and community. August isn't just selling products — it's cultivating a cultural conversation about periods, body autonomy, and breaking stigma. Nadya's transition from nonprofit leader to brand founder was a deliberate evolution: she realized that systemic change requires both advocacy *and* market innovation.

### Content Creator & Advocate

With millions of followers on TikTok and other platforms, Nadya has become one of the most visible voices in menstrual justice. She uses social media not just for promotion, but for education — breaking down stigma in real time, reaching audiences that traditional advocacy channels never could.

## Key Lessons for Young Asian Entrepreneurs

### 1. Start Before You Feel "Ready"
Nadya launched a national nonprofit at 16. She didn't wait for a degree, funding, or a mentor's blessing. If you see a problem, begin solving it — credibility comes from action, not credentials.

### 2. Turn Personal Pain into Purpose
Her experience with housing instability wasn't just hardship — it became the lens through which she saw a systemic gap. For many Asian entrepreneurs, cultural pressures or family expectations can feel like obstacles; reframing them as fuel for your mission changes everything.

### 3. Navigate Cultural Taboos Strategically
Period stigma is deeply rooted in many Asian cultures. Nadya didn't ignore the taboo — she confronted it with empathy, education, and community. When your venture touches a culturally sensitive topic, lead with understanding, not confrontation.

### 4. Bridge Nonprofit and For-Profit Worlds
Systemic change often requires both advocacy *and* market innovation. Nadya's arc from PERIOD → *Period Power* → August shows how one mission can express itself through multiple vehicles. Don't limit yourself to one model.

### 5. Use Storytelling as Infrastructure
Whether through a book, a brand, or social content, Nadya treats storytelling as a core business function — not a side channel. For young entrepreneurs, narrative is strategy: how you tell your story shapes who joins your mission.
